AI Decision Dashboard

A self-initiated concept exploring how fintech platforms can make AI-driven fraud signals legible to human analysts.

Background

The problem with AI in fintech

Fraud detection in financial services increasingly relies on machine learning models that flag suspicious transactions in real time. These systems can process thousands of signals simultaneously, far beyond what a human analyst could manage manually.

But the people working alongside these systems face a persistent problem: the AI flags a transaction as high risk, but provides no explanation of why. Analysts are left choosing between two bad options. They either trust the model blindly, or they ignore it and rely entirely on their own judgement. Both approaches undermine the value of the system.

This project is a self-initiated concept exploring how a fraud analyst's dashboard could be redesigned to close that gap, making AI signals interpretable, actionable, and trustworthy.

Research

Understanding the analyst's workflow

Secondary research into fraud analyst workflows, combined with public case studies from fintech companies, revealed a consistent pattern. Analysts who used AI-assisted tools reported two dominant frustrations: alert fatigue from too many low-quality flags, and a lack of explainability that made it hard to justify decisions to compliance teams.

A review of existing fraud dashboards found that most prioritised volume metrics over decision support. They were designed to report on what the AI had done, not to help the analyst decide what to do next.

R.01

Workflow research

Mapped a typical fraud analyst's daily workflow using public documentation, job descriptions, and fintech product teardowns to understand the decision sequence from alert to action.

R.02

Dashboard audit

Reviewed publicly available screenshots and demos of fraud platforms including Feedzai, Sardine, and Stripe Radar. Identified where explainability was absent and where cognitive load was highest.

R.03

Signal prioritisation

Researched the most common signals used in transaction fraud detection to understand what information analysts actually need to evaluate a flagged transaction.

Findings

What the research revealed

  • Analysts needed to understand the risk score, not just see it. A number without reasoning created more hesitation, not less.
  • Speed and accuracy were in tension. Dashboards that showed everything slowed analysts down. The design needed to support fast scanning with deeper detail available on demand.
  • The action moment was underdesigned. Most dashboards showed risk but made the "block" or "review" action feel disconnected from the reasoning that preceded it.
Key insight

Analysts didn't need the AI to make decisions for them. They needed to understand the AI's reasoning well enough to make a confident, defensible decision themselves. The dashboard's job was to close that gap.

Design Goals

What the dashboard needed to do

G.01

Make risk scores interpretable, not just visible

A score of 87 means nothing without context. The design needed to show what signals drove it and how they combined.

G.02

Support fast triage without sacrificing depth

Analysts reviewing dozens of alerts needed to scan quickly. Detail should be available immediately when they need to go deeper, without cluttering the default view.

G.03

Connect reasoning to action

The "block" and "review" options should sit directly alongside the evidence that justifies them. The decision and the reasoning needed to live in the same view.

G.04

Reduce alert fatigue through better prioritisation

Surface the highest-risk flags clearly. Don't treat every alert with equal visual weight when the stakes are clearly different.

Process

Structuring the information

The core design challenge was information hierarchy. Three structural approaches were explored in wireframes. The first showed all transaction data in a flat table, which made scanning fast but provided no explanation of risk. The second used a master/detail pattern with a fixed side panel, which was clearer but required too many clicks to get from alert to action. The third combined both: a scannable transaction list on the left with a persistent, contextual alert panel on the right that updated based on the selected row.

This third approach became the direction. It allowed analysts to triage quickly while keeping the reasoning always in view for the selected transaction.

Solution

The dashboard

The final design centres on a single analyst view combining a real-time transaction list, KPI strip, and a persistent alert detail panel. Risk scores are broken down into contributing signal weights. The action buttons sit directly below the evidence that justifies them.

Reflection

What this project taught me

The hardest design decision was how much information to show in the default view. Showing every signal for every transaction would overwhelm analysts reviewing dozens of alerts a day. Hiding too much would undermine the explainability the entire concept was built around.

The solution was selective emphasis: the transaction list shows only the risk score, while the detail panel shows the full breakdown for the selected transaction. Depth is always one click away, never buried.

Placing the action buttons directly below the signal breakdown. Connecting the reasoning to the action in a single view made the decision feel supported rather than arbitrary.

With real analyst access I'd test whether the score breakdown is read in the order intended, and whether the signal labels are phrased in a way analysts already recognise from their own mental models.

Designing for high-stakes decisions means designing for the moment when the human takes over from the AI. That handoff deserves as much attention as any other interaction in the flow.

Financial products sit at the intersection of data, trust, and consequential decisions. That combination is exactly where I find the most interesting design problems.
