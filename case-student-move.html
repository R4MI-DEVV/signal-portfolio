Background

The problem with moving

Moving to a new city for university is one of the most consequential transitions a young person makes. The stakes are high, financially, emotionally, and practically. But the tools available to students navigating this transition are fragmented, unverified, and often designed for a very different audience.

The housing market doesn't slow down for newcomers. Scammers know this. Students who don't know the city, don't know landlord norms, and don't have time to do deep research are the most vulnerable to housing fraud, and the most underserved by existing platforms.

⚠️

Housing scams

Fraudulent listings targeting students unfamiliar with local rental norms and pricing.

🗺️

No local knowledge

Students can't evaluate neighborhoods they've never visited, what's safe, walkable, or student-friendly.

📄

Confusing rental processes

Lease terms, deposits, and tenant rights vary widely and are rarely explained clearly.

🕳️

Unverified advice

Students rely on social media groups for housing tips, unmoderated, unverified and often wrong.

Research

What students actually experience

User interviews with students who had recently relocated revealed something important: the anxiety wasn't just about finding housing, it was about not knowing who to trust. They described a constant background fear of being deceived, combined with the pressure to make decisions quickly before listings disappeared.

Competitive analysis showed a clear gap. Platforms like Blocket and Facebook Marketplace have the listings but no trust signals. University housing lists are verified but extremely limited in supply. Nothing bridged the two.

R.01

User interviews (8 participants)

Students who had relocated in the past 12 months, across 3 Swedish cities. Sessions focused on the emotional arc of the relocation process, not just the logistics.

R.02

Competitive landscape review

Mapped 12 housing platforms against student-specific needs: scam detection, peer reviews, neighborhood data, and checklist support. None covered all four.

R.03

Scam pattern analysis

Reviewed documented student housing scams to identify consistent patterns in fraudulent listings, pricing, language, communication style, and payment requests.

Findings

What the research revealed

Three patterns appeared consistently across interviews:

  • Students wanted to feel confident, not just safe. There's a difference between "this is probably fine" and "I understand why this is trustworthy."
  • Checklists reduced anxiety dramatically, even when students already knew what to do. The act of externalising the process lowered cognitive load.
  • Peer validation mattered more than expert validation. Reviews from former students carried more weight than official ratings or platform badges.
Key insight

Trust isn't built with a safety score alone. Students needed to understand why something was flagged, not just that it was. Transparency about reasoning was more reassuring than a confident verdict.

Design Goals

What the solution needed to achieve

G.01

Reduce cognitive load during a high-stress decision

The experience should feel calm and structured, not like another thing to figure out.

G.02

Make risk visible without creating panic

Flag concerns clearly, but always with context. The goal is informed confidence, not heightened anxiety.

G.03

Build trust through transparency, not authority

Explain how conclusions were reached. Students should understand the reasoning, not just receive a verdict.

G.04

Design for users with no local knowledge

Assume the user has never been to the city. Every piece of information should be interpretable without prior context.

Process

From sketches to screens

Early sketches explored three interaction models: a search-first approach (like Airbnb), a feed-first approach (like Blocket), and a checklist-first approach that frontloaded the relocation process. User testing in lo-fi Figma prototypes pointed strongly toward the third. Students said starting with the checklist made the whole task feel manageable.

Three rounds of iteration focused primarily on the scam detection UI. The first version used a red/amber/green system that users found anxiety-inducing. The second replaced colour with plain-language explanations. The third combined both, colour for quick scanning, language for understanding.

Solution

MoveWise - key screens

The final design centres on three core views: the listing trust analysis, the neighborhood comparison, and the relocation checklist. Each is designed to reduce a specific kind of uncertainty that students identified in research.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

The main tension in this project was the line between being helpful and being alarming. Flagging potential scams too aggressively made users anxious; flagging too gently risked missing real danger. That balance came from testing, not from design intuition alone.

Combining transparent reasoning with visual signals. Users didn't just want to know something was risky, they wanted to understand why, in plain language.

Earlier testing with users in active housing searches, not retrospective interviews. The emotional state of someone actively searching is fundamentally different from someone recalling the experience.

The checklist was the highest-value feature, yet it was almost cut in early scope discussions. Sometimes the most calming intervention is the simplest one.

Designing for high-stakes, emotionally charged decisions requires a different kind of restraint. The designer's job is to reduce noise, not add more signals.
