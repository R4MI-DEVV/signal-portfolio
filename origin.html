Introduction

Who I am today

I'm Ikram Hussein, a UX designer with an engineering mindset. I'm about to begin studying Chemical Engineering at KTH, and in parallel I design digital products that help people make better decisions: when they're uncertain, when the stakes are high, or when the information in front of them is more complex than it needs to be.

My background bridges both the technical and human sides of problem-solving. At Volvo Cars I worked inside a data and AI team, gaining hands-on experience with real systems and real data. In my own projects I focus on the people who use those systems. I care about the reasoning behind every design decision as much as the final result.

Origin

Where this started

At Lindholmens Tekniska Gymnasium I studied the Technology Programme, where I first encountered web development and found something that stuck. Building things that actually worked felt like a precise, satisfying language for solving precise problems.

But the more I built, the more I noticed that technically correct wasn't the same as useful. A feature could work perfectly and still confuse the person it was meant to help. That gap, between what a system does and what people actually need, became the thing I wanted to understand.

I didn't move away from engineering. I moved toward a different kind of problem-solving, one where the system to understand is human behaviour, not code.

I started studying UX design self-directed, working through Figma, research methods, and interaction design principles. Then in early 2026, through Tekniksprånget, I had the opportunity to apply that thinking inside one of Sweden's leading engineering companies.

Volvo Cars, Data & AI Hub, Industrial Operations

Through Tekniksprånget I joined Volvo Cars' Data & AI Hub team within Industrial Operations from February to May 2026. I gained direct insight into how data and AI are used to support operational processes and decision-making at scale. Working with Python, Excel, Power BI, Streamlit, and SQL through practical assignments, I learned how to work with real data in a corporate environment and collaborate across teams. That experience sharpened something important: the harder it is for people to interpret information, the more consequential every design decision becomes.

Design Principles

How I think

Rather than listing skills, I'd rather explain the philosophy behind how I approach design problems. Three principles show up consistently across every project.

01. Investigate

Find the real problem

Real design problems are rarely what they appear at first. Before sketching anything, I need to understand who's experiencing the problem and what's actually causing it, not just what's visible on the surface.

02. Structure

Map what matters

Complexity doesn't disappear. It gets organised. Patterns in research reveal patterns in behaviour, which reveal the structure a design can follow. I spend more time mapping than most people expect.

03. Refine

Iterate toward clarity

Good design is earned through iteration, not arrived at in the first pass. I treat each test and each piece of feedback as data, something to be interpreted rather than just acted on.

What's next

KTH, Chemical Engineering

In 2026 I begin a BSc in Chemical Engineering at Kungliga Tekniska Högskolan. To some that might seem like a departure from design, but the pattern I see is the same. Chemical engineering is about understanding complex systems, identifying the variables that matter, and designing processes that produce reliable outcomes.

Every field I've moved into has brought new kinds of complexity and new kinds of people to design for. The approach doesn't change: understand the system, understand the person, reduce the distance between them.
